This is a classic multi-tier commission and attribution problem. When you have a mix of direct agents and sub-agents reporting to internal sales staff, things can get incredibly messy if you rely on spreadsheets.

To solve this, you need to build or configure a dedicated Agent Tracking System (ATS). Since you are dealing with EPC or installation-style projects where a lead eventually converts into a finalized physical deal, the tracking needs to be directly tied to the project's lifecycle.

Here is the deep dive into how to architect this solution from both a business and system perspective:

1. The Relational Structure (The Foundation)
To track who gets paid what, you need a strict relational hierarchy in your database.

Users/Roles: * Admin/Company (Has global view and payout authority)

Sales Person (Internal employee)

Agent / Channel Partner (External referrer)

The Mapping: Every Agent must have a ReportsTo field. This field either maps to a specific Sales Person ID, or it is left blank/maps to the Company directly. This solves the hierarchy issue.

The Leads: Every Lead generated must have a mandatory GeneratedBy field linking back to the specific Agent ID.

2. The Business Workflow
You need to establish a strict chain of custody for every lead so that commissions are automated rather than calculated manually.

Lead Capture: Give every agent a unique referral code, a dedicated web form, or a portal login to submit leads. If a Sales Person enters a lead manually on behalf of their agent, they must select that agent from a dropdown.

Deal Conversion: The lead goes through your standard pipeline (e.g., Prospecting -> Site Visit -> Quoting -> Installation Complete).

Commission Trigger: Commissions should never be calculated on raw leads. The system should only generate a "Commission Owed" record when the Deal Status changes to Completed or Payment Received.

Flexible Commission Rates: Build a table for commission rules. Some agents might have a flat rate, others might have a percentage based on the system size or project value. The system must lock in the commission rate at the time the deal is closed.

3. The Ledger System (Tracking Payouts)
Since one agent might bring in 50 leads and another might bring in 1, you need a ledger system, not just a spreadsheet.

Wallet / Ledger Table: Every agent has a digital "ledger" in the system.

Credits: When an agent's lead turns into a completed installation, the system automatically credits their ledger with the calculated commission amount.

Debits: When the company accounts team actually transfers the money to the agent's bank account, they log a "Payout" in the system, which deducts from the ledger balance.

Result: You always know exactly how much "Unpaid Commission" the company currently owes to every single agent at any given second.

4. Visibility and Dashboards
Transparency stops agents from constantly calling the company to ask about their money.

Agent Dashboard: They should log in and see three things: Leads Submitted, Deals Closed, and Current Unpaid Earnings.

Sales Person Dashboard: They see the performance of all the agents assigned to them.

Admin Dashboard: The finance team sees a master list of all approved payouts that need to be sent to the bank.

By structuring the data this way, it doesn't matter if an agent reports to a sales guy or directly to the firm. The system just looks at the Lead -> Agent connection, checks the completion status, and credits the ledger.
